How Laser Printers Work

CompTIA A+ 220-1201 - Core 1 | Lesson 3.8

This topic is part of the CompTIA A+ Core 1 (220-1201) Study Guide study guide.

Overview of Laser Printers

  • Laser printers are high-speed, high-capacity output devices that use high voltage, charged ions, and powdered toner to produce printed pages.
  • Key advantages include fast printing speeds and high-quality output.
  • Challenges include complexity due to many moving parts, the need for memory and CPU to render pages, and potential toner spills that create messes.

Laser Printing Process

  • The printing process involves transferring an image from the printer’s memory to a photosensitive drum and then to paper.
  • Step 1: Charging the Drum
    • The photosensitive drum (also called the OPC drum or organic photoconductor drum) is given a negative charge using a corona wire or a charged roller.
  • Step 2: Writing the Image
    • A laser writes the image onto the drum by removing the negative charge in specific areas, creating an electrostatic image.
  • Step 3: Applying Toner
    • Negatively charged toner is applied to the drum and sticks only to the areas where the laser removed the charge.
    • Since the drum and toner are both negatively charged, toner does not stick to unexposed areas.
  • Step 4: Transferring Toner to Paper
    • A sheet of paper passes by the drum, and the toner is transferred from the drum to the paper.
  • Step 5: Fusing the Toner
    • The paper passes through a fuser, which uses heat and pressure to permanently bond the toner to the page.
  • Step 6: Cleaning the Drum
    • Any remaining toner is cleaned off the drum, and the process repeats for the next page.

Toner Cartridges and OPC Drums

  • Toner cartridges contain powdered toner and may include the OPC drum or have it as a separate component.
  • OPC drums are light-sensitive and must be stored in light-proof bags until installation to prevent damage.
  • Replacing toner cartridges:
    • Power down the printer, open the cover, and remove the old cartridge.
    • Remove protective strips or packing materials from the new cartridge.
    • Insert the new cartridge and restart the printer.
  • Color laser printers use multiple cartridges (e.g., cyan, magenta, yellow, black), each installed in its designated slot.

Maintenance and Troubleshooting

  • Maintenance kits include replacement parts that wear out over time, such as:
    • Feed rollers
    • Fuser units
  • Page counters track usage and indicate when maintenance is needed based on manufacturer recommendations.
  • Installing a maintenance kit:
    • Power down the printer and replace components as specified.
    • Reset the page counter to monitor usage for the next maintenance cycle.
  • Printer calibration ensures consistent toner density:
    • Print test pages and adjust settings if output is too light or too dark.
    • Calibration may be automated or require manual adjustments.

Cleaning and Safety

  • Toner handling:
    • Toner is a fine dust that can easily become airborne; handle with care.
    • Avoid using compressed air to clean toner spills, as it disperses toner into the air.
    • Use a toner-specific vacuum or wipe away dust with a damp cloth.
  • Cleaning components:
    • Use cold water or isopropyl alcohol (IPA) for cleaning internal parts.
    • Avoid harsh chemicals or hot water, which can damage components or melt toner.
    • For external cleaning, use a damp cloth with cold water.
  • Safety precautions:
    • The fuser unit becomes extremely hot during operation; allow it to cool before handling.
    • If toner gets on skin, wash with cold water (hot water can melt toner, making it harder to remove).

Common Issues and Solutions

  • Low toner: Output becomes lighter; replace the toner cartridge when empty.
  • Toner spills: Clean carefully to avoid spreading toner dust.
  • Worn-out parts: Replace components like rollers or fusers using a maintenance kit.
  • Density issues: Perform a printer calibration to adjust toner output.
