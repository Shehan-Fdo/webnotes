Fiber Optic Cabling

CompTIA A+ 220-1201 - Core 1 | Lesson 3.2

This topic is part of the CompTIA A+ Core 1 (220-1201) Study Guide study guide.

Overview of Fiber Optic Technology

  • Used in data centers and high-speed networks to transmit information via light instead of electrical signals (copper).
  • Advantages over copper cabling:
    • Immune to electromagnetic interference (EMI) and radio frequency interference (RFI).
    • More secure—difficult to tap without specialized equipment.
    • Signals degrade less over distance, allowing transmission over kilometers without regeneration.
    • Ideal for environments with industrial equipment or high interference.
  • Requires specialized equipment for installation, monitoring, and maintenance.

Structure of Fiber Optic Cable

  • Composed of multiple layers for protection and signal integrity:
    • Core: High-refractive-index material where light travels.
    • Cladding: Low-refractive-index layer surrounding the core to reflect light back into the core via total internal reflection.
    • Buffer coating: Protective layer shielding the core and cladding from physical damage.
  • Light sources:
    • LED: Commonly used for short-range transmissions (e.g., multimode fiber).
    • Laser: Used for long-range transmissions (e.g., single-mode fiber).

Fiber Optic Connectors

  • Designed to protect the fragile fiber core and ensure precise alignment.
    • Ferrule: Ceramic component at the connector’s center that holds and aligns the fiber.
    • Visible as a small discoloration in the center of the connector.
  • Common connector types vary by shape (e.g., square, round) and application.

Types of Fiber Optic Cables

  • Multimode Fiber (MMF):
    • Used for short-range communication (e.g., within buildings).
    • Typical distance: up to 2 kilometers.
    • Uses LED light sources due to lower cost and shorter range requirements.
    • Larger core diameter allows light to travel in multiple paths (modes), causing signal dispersion over long distances.
  • Single-Mode Fiber (SMF):
    • Designed for long-range communication (e.g., 100+ kilometers without regeneration).
    • Uses laser light sources for precise, high-speed transmission.
    • Narrow core diameter ensures light travels in a single path (mode), reducing signal loss and dispersion.

Key Considerations for Fiber Optics

  • Multiple fiber types and connectors exist—choose based on distance, speed, and environment.
  • Installation and maintenance require specialized tools and training.
  • More expensive than copper cabling but offers superior performance for high-demand applications.
