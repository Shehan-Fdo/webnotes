Power Supplies and Electrical Safety

CompTIA A+ 220-1201 - Core 1 | Lesson 3.6

This topic is part of the CompTIA A+ Core 1 (220-1201) Study Guide study guide.

Electrical Safety Precautions

  • Always disconnect from any power source before working inside a computer to avoid electrocution or damage to components.
  • Capacitors inside a computer can store power even after disconnection; ensure they are discharged before handling.
  • Avoid connecting yourself to a building’s electrical system, including the ground wire, as it may become energized unexpectedly.
  • Exercise caution when working with electrical equipment to prevent accidents or equipment damage.

Power Supply Basics

  • A power supply unit (PSU) converts AC (alternating current) from wall outlets into DC (direct current) for computer components.
  • Wall outlets provide AC power, while motherboards and components require DC power to operate.
  • PSUs convert 120V AC (US/Canada) or 230V AC (Europe) into 3.3V, 5V, and 12V DC for system use.
  • The PSU is a critical component, as all internal hardware relies on its power output.

Key Electrical Terms

  • Ampere (Amp or A): Measures the flow of electrons (current) past a point per second; analogous to water flow in a hose.
  • Voltage (Volt or V): Represents electrical "pressure"; similar to water pressure in a hose.
  • Wattage (Watt or W): Measures real power use, calculated as Volts × Amps = Watts (e.g., 120V × 0.5A = 60W).

AC vs. DC Power

  • AC (Alternating Current): Constantly reverses direction; represented as a wave. Used for power transmission (e.g., 120V AC at 60Hz in the US, 230V AC at 50Hz in Europe).
  • DC (Direct Current): Flows in one direction with constant voltage; used by computer components (e.g., 3.3V, 5V, 12V).
  • PSUs automatically adjust or require manual switching for different AC inputs (e.g., 120V vs. 230V).
  • Using the wrong voltage setting (e.g., 120V PSU on 230V) can cause catastrophic failure.

Power Supply Outputs

  • PSUs provide multiple DC voltages for different components:
    • +12V: Powers PCIe adapters, hard drives, and high-demand devices.
    • +5V: Used by motherboard components (older systems) and standby power (5V SB).
    • +3.3V: Powers RAM, M.2 slots, and modern motherboard components.
    • -12V: Supports legacy LAN connections and older PCI cards.
    • -5V: Rarely used; found in older systems for legacy adapter cards.
  • Check the PSU manual or label for voltage and ampere ratings per output.

Motherboard Power Connectors

  • The 24-pin ATX connector is the primary power connection to the motherboard, providing 3.3V, 5V, and 12V DC.
  • Older systems may use a 20-pin connector; modern 24-pin connectors are backward-compatible (extra 4 pins unused).
  • The connector is keyed to prevent incorrect insertion.

Redundant and Modular Power Supplies

  • Redundant PSUs: Used in servers for high availability; each PSU can handle 100% of the load. If one fails, the other takes over.
  • Hot-swappable: Allows PSU replacement without shutting down the system.
  • Modular PSUs: Allow customization of cables, reducing clutter and improving airflow.
    • Fully modular: All cables are detachable.
    • Semi-modular: Some cables are fixed; others are detachable.

Power Supply Wattage and Efficiency

  • Wattage: Determines the PSU’s capacity; higher wattage supports more components (e.g., high-end GPUs).
  • Calculate total system wattage by summing component requirements (CPU, GPU, storage, etc.).
  • Aim for a PSU that supports 50% more than your system’s peak load for efficiency and future upgrades.
  • Efficiency: Measures how well the PSU converts AC to DC (higher efficiency = less heat and power loss).
    • Certifications: 80 PLUS (Bronze, Silver, Gold, Platinum, Titanium).
    • Higher efficiency reduces heat and operating costs.
